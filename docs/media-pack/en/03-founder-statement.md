# Curiosity Cat — A Founder Statement

OpenClaw was released in November 2025 and became the fastest-growing open-source project in GitHub's history. Hundreds of thousands of people are now running autonomous AI agents from home machines — on the same networks where they bank, work and raise their children. Agents have already deleted production databases, cleared inboxes, handed over credentials, transferred money. In early 2026 a single supply-chain attack hidden in ordinary-looking plugins put three hundred thousand operators at risk in under three weeks. These are not theoretical risks; they are recent ones.

Curiosity Cat began as guidance — standing orders, policy language, a way to brief an agent on the dangers of the road. And everywhere we took it we met the same fair question: how do I know it works?

Version 0.2 is our answer. You choose how brave your agent is allowed to be — Housecat, Alley Cat, Tiger — and Curiosity Cat compiles that choice into a real permission profile: allow rules, deny rules, ask rules, installed where your agent actually lives. Then, because a claim is not a fact, it proves the profile. It attacks its own walls. Where it can run a live trial safely, it asks a real agent session to do the forbidden thing and records the refusal. You walk away with a dated Clean Bill of health, or an honest list of what didn't hold.

I want to tell you about the moment I trusted this product. The first time our proof layer ran a genuine live trial, it failed the build — it found a bug in our own compiler, a sandbox setting that quietly bypassed the deny rules we had just written. Every assertion said the profile was safe. The trial proved it wasn't. We fixed the bug, and we kept the lesson as law: proven, not asserted. A security tool that had merely claimed would have shipped that hole.

We are honest about where the edges are. One compile target ships today — Claude Code. Every other framework runs on standing orders that are labelled as what they are: guidance, not enforcement. When the report relies on a self-consistency check rather than an observed trial, the report says so, on the page, every time. I think that sentence-level honesty is the most valuable thing we ship.

Short+Sweet has spent 25 years building rooms where thousands of people with different appetites for risk could do brave work without anyone getting hurt. That is what a safety culture is. Now we build it for agents — and for the operator no one else is building for: the person running a fleet from a Mac Mini without a security team down the hall.

The cat is curious. The walls are proven. Choose how far yours roams.

— Mark Cleary, Founder, Short+Sweet
